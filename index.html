Flexibility

The thing that bends is the thing that lasts

Welcome

5 to 8 minAbout today's group

Today we're talking about flexibility. Not the yoga kind. The kind that shows up in how you think, how you act, and how you handle it when life does something you didn't plan for.

Here's the thing about eating disorders: they love rigidity. They love rules, routines, and the feeling that everything is under control. Flexibility is what the disorder is most afraid of, because flexible people are harder to control.

Confidentiality

What's shared here stays here. Names, stories, details. All protected. The one exception is safety: if there's risk of harm, that changes things. Outside of that, this space is yours.

You can pass at any point. No explanation needed. If something doesn't fit right now, say "pass" and we move on.

2. Experiential exercise

Sit up straight. Like, really straight. Hold it.

1
Notice what's happening in your body right now. Tension, effort, holding.
This is rigidity. It takes a lot of energy to maintain.
2
Now let your body find a position that's comfortable but still upright. Let your shoulders drop. Unclench your jaw.
This is flexibility. You're still here, still engaged. But it costs less.
3
Now slump completely. Let everything go.
This is collapse. It feels easy at first but you can't function from here.
4
Come back to the middle. That middle place, between rigid and collapsed, is what we're working on today.
The concept

15 to 20 minWhat flexibility actually means

Flexibility is not about having no structure. It's not about saying yes to everything or giving up your preferences. It's the ability to adapt when the situation calls for it without losing yourself in the process.

Think of it like a tree in a storm. A rigid tree snaps. A flexible tree bends, takes the hit, and comes back. A tree with no structure at all just falls over.

The spectrum

Flexibility lives in the middle. Tap each zone to see what it looks like.

Rigid
Flexible
Chaotic

Rigid looks like: same routine no matter what, one "right" way to do things, falling apart when plans change, black-and-white thinking, rules that can never bend. It feels safe, but the safety costs a lot.

  • "I have to eat at exactly this time or the whole day is ruined"
  • "If I can't do it perfectly, I won't do it at all"
  • "I already decided how I feel about this and nothing will change my mind"

Flexible looks like: having preferences but adapting when needed, trying new things even when they're uncomfortable, holding two ideas at the same time, adjusting without falling apart. It's not comfortable. It's functional.

  • "This isn't what I planned, and I can work with it"
  • "I feel anxious about this AND I'm going to try it anyway"
  • "I was wrong about that, and that's actually fine"

Chaotic looks like: no structure at all, reactive to everything, no consistent values or direction, saying yes to everything, impulsive without reflection. It feels like freedom but there's nothing to hold onto.

  • "Whatever, nothing matters anyway"
  • "I don't care, I'll just do whatever"
  • "Rules are stupid, I don't need any"

Most people with eating disorders live on the rigid end. Recovery is not about jumping to chaos. It's about learning to live closer to the middle.

Four types of flexibility

Where bending matters most

Cognitive flexibility
+

The ability to think in more than two categories. Rigid thinking sounds like "always" and "never" and "the only option." Flexible thinking sounds like "maybe," "it depends," and "what if."

  • Rigid: "I'm either doing great or I'm a failure"
  • Flexible: "I'm struggling with one thing and doing okay with another. Both are real."
  • Rigid: "If they're mad at me, they hate me"
  • Flexible: "They might be mad about this one thing and still care about me overall"
The eating disorder is a machine that turns flexible thoughts into rigid ones. Recovery is learning to notice the machine at work.
Behavioral flexibility
+

The ability to try a different response instead of running the same autopilot every time. Not forcing yourself. Choosing.

  • Eating something you didn't plan to eat because the situation changed
  • Saying yes to something spontaneous even though it wasn't on the schedule
  • Doing a social thing when the urge is to isolate
  • Resting when the urge is to push harder
Behavioral flexibility is not about forcing yourself to be uncomfortable all the time. It's about having more than one option available when you need it.
Emotional flexibility
+

The ability to let emotions move through you instead of getting stuck on one or shutting them all down. Emotions are supposed to shift and change. When they get stuck, that's rigidity.

  • Letting yourself feel sad without deciding you're depressed forever
  • Feeling angry without needing to act on it right now
  • Being anxious about something and not letting the anxiety make the decision for you
  • Feeling two things at once and letting both be true
Emotional rigidity looks like getting stuck in one feeling for days, or numbing everything so you don't feel any of it. Emotional flexibility is the middle.
Identity flexibility
+

The ability to let who you are evolve. This one is hard. Especially when you're young, there's pressure to define yourself and then stay that definition forever. But identity is not a tattoo. It's more like a playlist that you keep updating.

  • "I'm the quiet one" doesn't mean you can never be loud
  • "I'm the strong one" doesn't mean you can never need help
  • "I'm the sick one" doesn't mean that has to be your whole story
  • You can outgrow a version of yourself and still honor who that person was
The eating disorder gives you an identity. It tells you who you are. Letting go of that identity is terrifying because it means facing the question: who am I without it? The answer is: someone you haven't fully met yet. And that's not a bad thing.
Practice

15 to 20 minThe rigidity audit

Six areas where rigidity likes to hide. Tap each one to see the rigid pattern and the flexible alternative. Be honest about which ones you recognize.

Food
"It has to be this exact thing at this exact time"
What if today you let the time shift by 15 minutes, or swapped one thing, and noticed what actually happens?
Body
"I have to check, measure, or compare every day"
What if you skipped the check for one day and paid attention to how your body feels instead of how it looks?
Social
"I already know how this will go, so why bother"
What if you went into one social situation this week without a prediction? Just see what happens.
School / Work
"If it's not perfect, it's not worth turning in"
What if you submitted something at 80% and noticed whether the world actually ended?
Routine
"If my routine gets disrupted, the whole day is ruined"
What if the disruption is the interesting part? What if you could recover from a change without starting over?
Identity
"I'm just not the kind of person who does that"
What if you tried being that kind of person for one afternoon? You can always come back.

You don't have to share which ones hit. Just notice. If something lands, sit with it for a second.

Guided exercise

The flexibility experiment

Pick one area from above (or any area in your life where you notice rigidity). Walk through these steps in your head or on paper.

1
What's the rigid rule or pattern?
Say it plainly. "I always..." or "I never..." or "It has to be..."
2
What does the rule protect you from?
Anxiety? Uncertainty? Judgment? Feeling out of control? Name it.
3
What does the rule cost you?
Time? Energy? Connection? Experiences? Fun? Be specific.
4
What would bending this rule by 10% look like?
Not breaking it. Bending it. The smallest possible flex.
5
What do you predict would happen if you tried?
Write down the prediction. Then, if you actually try it, check whether the prediction was right.

Facilitator note

Flexibility is not about being chill. It's not about not caring. It's about being able to hold structure and still adapt. That's harder than rigidity. Rigidity is easy because it removes choice. Flexibility means you have to keep choosing, over and over, and that takes courage. This week, try one small bend. Not a break. A bend. Notice what happens in your body when you do it, and notice whether the thing you were afraid of actually happens. That information is yours to keep.

Weekly challenge

Pick one rule you follow on autopilot. Bend it by 10% on one day. Before you do it, write down your prediction of what will happen. After you do it, write down what actually happened. Bring the results next week if you want to share.
